0. Abstract

The Ω(t) cosmological model in a nutshell: Imagine that everything we know about the current universe relies on a model with some limitations: the so-called standard model (ΛCDM). It works well, but it invokes mysterious entities like dark energy or dark matter, whose nature remains poorly understood. To bypass these mysteries, the Ω(t) cosmological model proposes something original:

  • 1. A new way of thinking about the universe: The standard model says the universe has been expanding continuously since the Big Bang. Here, the Ω(t) model instead suggests that the universe has a closed topology: imagine a giant sphere. Instead of perpetual expansion, one would have a retraction, or at least a different dynamics, involving a certain form of contraction followed by a bounce (“causal bounce”).
  • 2. The central idea: Ω(t), a special field that modifies the fundamental constants. In this model, there exists a “scalar field” called Ω(t). Think of this field as an invisible fluid present everywhere, evolving slowly over time. This field would directly affect two fundamental constants of physics, normally assumed fixed: the speed of light (c) and the gravitational constant (G). These constants would no longer be frozen, but would vary slightly depending on Ω(t).
  • 3. Practical consequences of the model: This slow, smooth variation could explain phenomena that are difficult to understand with the standard model, such as anomalies observed in the cosmic microwave background (the relic radiation from the Big Bang). It could also resolve certain complex cosmological problems, such as the origin of large-scale structures like galaxies and clusters.
  • 4. Predictions and experimental tests: The Ω(t) model is not just a nice idea: it proposes concrete tests. For example: test predictions by observing specific astronomical phenomena (primordial nucleosynthesis, binary pulsars). Verify the predicted evolution of fundamental constants through precise experiments.
  • 5. A cyclic universe with causal bounce: Finally, an intriguing aspect is that this model predicts a universe capable of undergoing contraction phases followed by a bounce, potentially leading to a cyclic scenario, or at least to a different phase prior to what we currently call the Big Bang. In short, the Ω(t) cosmological model attempts to simplify and unify our understanding of the universe while providing an elegant explanation for some of today’s mysteries. It is only a hypothesis to be further explored, but it is original and potentially testable.

1. Introduction

  • Limits of $\Lambda$CDM: reliance on undetected dark energy/matter, ad hoc inflation, $H_0$ and $S_8$ tensions, CMB anomalies.
  • Motivation: metric/constant unification via a single field $\Omega(t)$; a cyclic scenario avoiding a singularity.
  • Central idea: slow variations of $c(t)$ and $G(t)$ induced by $\Omega(t)$, producing a metric retraction and a causal bounce.
Positioning note: aim to minimize exotic assumptions while remaining testable via BBN, CMB, LLR, binary pulsars, and spectroscopy-based bounds on constants.

2. Fundamental Assumptions

  • Closed universe ($k=+1$), FLRW metric: $\mathrm{d}s^2 = -c^2(t)\,\mathrm{d}t^2 + a^2(t)\,\gamma_{ij}\,\mathrm{d}x^i \mathrm{d}x^j$.
  • Scalar field $\Omega(t)$ with slow-roll evolution (potentially non-canonical kinetic term).
  • Dynamic functions: $c(\Omega)$, $G(\Omega)$, and possibly $\alpha(\Omega)$ via couplings.
  • Minimal matter coupling or conformal metric $\tilde g_{\mu\nu} = A^2(\Omega)\, g_{\mu\nu}$ (to be specified).
  • Temporal windows of validity: BBN consistency; local bounds ($\dot G/G$, $\dot \alpha/\alpha$).

3. Action and Equations of Motion

3.1 Effective action (general form)

$$ S = \int \! \mathrm{d}^4x \, \sqrt{-g} \left[ \frac{c^3(\Omega)}{16\pi \, G(\Omega)} \, R \; - \; \frac{1}{2} \, K(\Omega) \, g^{\mu\nu} \partial_{\mu}\Omega \, \partial_{\nu}\Omega \; - \; V(\Omega) \right] \; + \; S_{\mathrm{m}}\big[\psi,\, A^2(\Omega) g_{\mu\nu};\, c(\Omega)\big] . $$

Where $K(\Omega)$ is the kinetic function (allowing non-canonical cases), $V(\Omega)$ the potential, and a possible conformal coupling $A(\Omega)$ to matter fields $\psi$. The $c(\Omega)$ and $G(\Omega)$ dependences encode the dynamics of the “constants”.

3.2 Equations of motion

Variation with respect to $g_{\mu\nu}$ and $\Omega$:

$$ \frac{c^3}{8\pi G} G_{\mu\nu} + \dots = T^{(\Omega)}_{\mu\nu} + T^{(\mathrm{m})}_{\mu\nu}, \qquad \frac{1}{\sqrt{-g}} \partial_{\mu}\!\left( \sqrt{-g} \, K(\Omega) \, \partial^{\mu}\!\Omega \right) - V'(\Omega) + \mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{int}} = 0 . $$

Here $\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{int}}$ encodes source terms arising from the $c(\Omega)$ and $G(\Omega)$ dependences, and from the matter coupling $A(\Omega)$.

3.3 Modified Friedmann equations (FLRW, $k=+1$)

$$ H^2 \equiv \left(\frac{\dot a}{a}\right)^2 = \frac{8\pi G(\Omega)}{3}\,\rho_{\text{tot}} - \frac{c^2(\Omega)}{a^2} \; + \; \Delta_{\mathrm{var}}(\dot \Omega,\Omega), \\[4pt] \frac{\ddot a}{a} = -\frac{4\pi G(\Omega)}{3}\,(\rho_{\text{tot}}+3p_{\text{tot}}/c^2) \; + \; \Xi_{\mathrm{var}}(\dot \Omega, \ddot \Omega, \Omega). $$

The terms $\Delta_{\mathrm{var}}$ and $\Xi_{\mathrm{var}}$ collect corrections coming from derivatives of $\Omega$ and from the variations of $c$ and $G$.

3.4 Conservation / continuity equations

$$ \dot \rho_{\text{tot}} + 3H\,(\rho_{\text{tot}} + p_{\text{tot}}/c^2) = \mathcal{Q}(\Omega,\dot\Omega), $$

where $\mathcal{Q}$ parameterizes effective energy exchange due to varying “constants” and possible non-minimal couplings.

3.5 Field equation for $\Omega$ (homogeneous background)

$$ K(\Omega)\,\ddot \Omega + 3HK(\Omega)\,\dot \Omega + \tfrac{1}{2}K'(\Omega)\,\dot\Omega^2 + V'(\Omega) = J(\Omega; c', G', A'), $$

The source term $J$ gathers the contributions induced by $c(\Omega)$, $G(\Omega)$ and $A(\Omega)$.

Dimensions & normalization. Specify the convention ($c_0=1$ or not), the dimension of $\Omega$, and the normalization of $K$ and $V$ to ensure dimensional consistency.

4. Cosmological Solutions

4.1 Evolutionary Phases

  • Quasi–radiative phase: $\Omega$ in slow-roll, $c$ increases moderately, $G$ decreases slowly.
  • Apparent acceleration: effective growth of a “cosmological” term $\Lambda_{\text{eff}}(\Omega)$.
  • Critical transition: conditions for global collapse.
  • Causal bounce: vacuum pressure/non-canonical kinetic terms, possible effective sign change of $G(\Omega)$; effective inversion of the arrow of time.

4.2 Analytical Criteria

$$ H=0,\quad \dot H > 0 \;\; \Rightarrow \;\; \text{bounce}; \qquad \ddot a > 0\ \text{at the critical instant}. $$

Express $\dot H$ as a function of $\Omega$ and its derivatives to obtain an explicit bounce condition.

4.3 Examples of analytical closures

Toy choices for $(K,V,c,G)$: e.g. $K(\Omega)=1$, $V(\Omega)=V_0(1-e^{-\lambda \Omega})^2$, $c(\Omega)=c_0 e^{\beta \Omega}$, $G(\Omega)=G_0 e^{\gamma \Omega}$. Discuss stability and bounds.

7. Discussion

7.1 Strengths

  • No exotic dark matter/energy (reinterpretation through dynamics of constants).
  • Geometric unification via $\Omega$.
  • Cyclic scenario avoiding singularity.

7.2 Limitations & sensitive points

  • Choice of $K,V,c(\Omega),G(\Omega),A(\Omega)$: stability, causality, absence of Ostrogradskian instabilities.
  • Tight observational constraints (BBN, CMB, LLR).
  • Weak equivalence principle: Eötvös tests if non-minimal matter coupling.

7.3 Predictions & tests

  • Subtle deviations in ISW and $f\sigma_8$.
  • GW signatures (phase velocity, dispersion).
  • Secular measurable evolution of constants ($\dot G, \dot c, \dot\alpha$) at predicted levels.
